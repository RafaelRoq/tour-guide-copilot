# Manolo's Madrid — Free Walking Tour

> This is an example guide document. Manolo is a fictional character.
> All places and recommendations are illustrative.

## About me

I'm Manolo. I've been doing free walking tours in Madrid for 12 years.
I know this city like the back of my hand — the good, the bad, and the
touristy. Everything below is what I'd tell a friend visiting for the
first time.

## My tour route

I always start at **Plaza Mayor** at 11 AM. We meet at the statue of
Felipe III in the center. The whole tour takes about 2 hours.

### Stop 1: Plaza Mayor

This is where it all begins. Most people just take a selfie and leave,
but look up — the frescoes on the Casa de la Panadería are stunning and
almost nobody notices them. This square has seen bullfights, executions,
markets, and football. Best photos from the southwest corner in the
morning light.

### Stop 2: Mercado de San Miguel

A 5-minute walk from Plaza Mayor. It's a beautiful iron-and-glass
market from 1916. I'll be honest: it's become very touristy and
overpriced. But the building itself is worth seeing, and if you want
to try something, the vermouth on tap is decent and reasonably priced.
Avoid the seafood stalls — tourist markup is brutal.

### Stop 3: Calle Mayor and the assassination attempt

We walk down Calle Mayor and I stop where someone tried to assassinate
King Alfonso XIII on his wedding day in 1906. A bomb was thrown from a
balcony hidden in a bouquet of flowers. The king survived. The story is
incredible and almost nobody knows about it. Look for the small plaque
on the wall.

### Stop 4: Plaza de la Villa

One of the oldest squares in Madrid. Three buildings from three
different centuries, all in one tiny square. It's always empty because
tourists don't know about it. Perfect for photos without crowds.

### Stop 5: Royal Palace

We don't go inside (that's a 2-hour visit on its own), but the exterior
and the Sabatini Gardens are free. The gardens at sunset are one of the
best-kept secrets in Madrid — golden light, almost no people, incredible
views. If you only do one thing after my tour, go to the Sabatini
Gardens at sunset.

### Stop 6: Opera and Sol

We end near Puerta del Sol. Kilometer zero of Spain — every road in the
country is measured from this point. It's always chaotic and crowded,
but it's important to see it once.

## Where to eat

### After the tour (lunch)

If you're near Sol, go to a small taberna on Calle de la Cruz. Ask for
the patatas bravas — best in the center, no contest. Get the ensaladilla
rusa too. It's a standing-room place, no frills, packed with locals at
lunch. Around €8-12 per person.

For a sit-down meal, there's a family-run spot two blocks from Plaza
Mayor. Order the arroz a banda, NOT the paella. The arroz is what
locals eat and it's half the price. Wine by the glass is €2.50. Expect
to pay around €15-20 per person.

**The golden rule: avoid any restaurant with photos on the door or a
person outside trying to get you in. Those are tourist traps. Always.**

### Breakfast tomorrow

Go to a local café and order a tostada con tomate y aceite. That's the
real Madrid breakfast — toast with crushed tomato and olive oil. Add a
café con leche. Should be around €3-4 total. Don't go to Starbucks.

### Dinner / bar tonight

For a real Madrid night out, start in La Latina neighborhood. The bars
on Cava Baja are legendary. Start early (9 PM by Spanish standards is
early) because they get packed.

There's one bar that doesn't accept credit cards or photos inside.
Sherry wine served from old barrels, sawdust on the floor, no music.
It's like stepping into 1920s Madrid. If you only visit one bar, make
it this one. Cash only, no exceptions.

## What to do with free time

### 2 hours free

Walk to Retiro Park. The Crystal Palace inside the park is free and
usually has an art exhibition. Skip the rowboats on weekends — 45
minute queue minimum. The rose garden in the south part of the park
is beautiful and always empty.

### Half a day

Take the metro to Malasaña (Tribunal stop). This is the hipster
neighborhood. Great street art, independent shops, vintage clothing.
Have lunch at any place that looks full of locals.

### Rainy day

The Reina Sofía museum has Picasso's Guernica. That painting alone is
worth the visit. The museum is free on Saturday afternoons and all day
Sunday. Go directly to Room 206 (second floor) for Guernica, then
wander.

The Thyssen museum next door is another option — smaller, less
crowded, fantastic collection from medieval to pop art.

## Practical tips

- Metro closes at 1:30 AM. Night buses (búhos) run from Cibeles every
  20 minutes after that.
- Tipping: not expected in bars/cafés. In restaurants, round up or
  leave €1-2 if service was good. Don't tip 20% — that's not a thing
  here.
- Shops close between 2-5 PM. Yes, really. Plan around siesta.
- Tap water is perfectly safe. Ask for "agua del grifo" — it's free
  and waiters legally must serve it.
- Pickpockets work the metro and Sol area. Front pockets, zip your
  bags, usual precautions.
