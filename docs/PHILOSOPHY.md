# Philosophy: Copilot, not replacement

## The thesis

A tour guide's value isn't information. It's curation.

Any search engine can tell you the Royal Palace in Madrid was built in
the 18th century. Any chatbot can list the top-rated restaurants near
Sol. But none of them can tell you that the Sabatini Gardens at sunset
are one of the best-kept secrets in the city, or that at a certain
taberna you should order the arroz a banda instead of the paella because
that's what locals eat and it costs half as much.

That kind of knowledge — specific, opinionated, tested by years of
walking the same streets — is what makes a guide worth following. And
right now, that knowledge dies the moment the tour ends.

## The problem with current AI in tourism

Many AI tools in the tourism space follow the same logic: if the model
has enough data, it can replace the human. Build a good enough chatbot,
feed it reviews and articles, and tourists won't need a person walking
them through the city.

This logic misses what makes human guides irreplaceable:

**Specificity.** A guide doesn't just know where to eat. They know what
to order, where to sit, what time to go, and what to avoid. That level
of detail doesn't exist in any database.

**Curation.** A guide filters. Out of 200 restaurants in a neighborhood,
they recommend two — and they can tell you why. An algorithm ranks by
aggregate rating. A guide ranks by personal experience.

**Voice.** A guide has opinions, personality, humor. "Skip this, it's a
tourist trap" is more useful than a 4.2-star rating. And it's more
trustworthy, because it comes from someone who has nothing to gain from
sending you to the wrong place.

## What we believe

- AI is excellent at structuring knowledge. It's not good at generating
  the kind of specific, local, opinionated knowledge that guides have.
- The right use of AI in this context is to take what the guide already
  knows and make it available, structured, and accessible — not to
  replace it with generated content.
- The guide should remain the source of truth. The AI organizes; it
  never invents.
- If the guide hasn't mentioned something, the system should say so,
  not fill the gap with generic suggestions.

## Why this matters beyond tourism

This isn't just about tour guides. The same pattern applies to any
expert whose knowledge is valuable but trapped in oral form: teachers,
consultants, craftspeople, coaches, doctors explaining treatment plans.

The question is always the same: can AI capture and structure what an
expert knows, without pretending to be the expert?

We think the answer is yes — and this project is our attempt to prove
it, starting with tour guides.
